Synthwave Horizon TIC-80

8 KB Lua · 60fps · animated loop

The first version. A pixel cart that runs at 60fps — the sun half-set, the perspective grid scrolling, the stars twinkling on a fixed rate. Time is built in: every frame is a different frame. The medium's power is exactly that. The medium's cost is that nothing here is still.

Horizon (SVG) SVG

2.3 KB · vector · static (or pulsing)

Same composition, drawn in geometry. A real <circle> for the sun, real <rect> cutouts for the stripes, real lines for the perspective grid. The medium's power is that it scales to any size without pixelation — the viewBox is the only resolution. The cost: nothing animates unless you make it animate. By default it is still.

Horizon (ASCII) ASCII

1,346 bytes · monospaced text · zero dependencies

The same horizon, drawn in 24 lines of monospaced text. # for the sun, | and - for the perspective grid, * and · for the stars. The medium's power is portability: this file renders identically in a 1980s vt100, a 2026 browser, and cat piped through less on a server. The cost: no gradients, no curves, no color. The illusion of a setting sun is in the widths of the stripe cutouts.

What the medium hides

On time

The TIC-80 cart is time. Every frame is computed; the sun's position is a function of t; the stars twinkle on a per-frame probability. There is no "first frame" of the cart, only the loop it lives inside. You cannot look at the cart; you watch it. The SVG and the ASCII, by contrast, are stills. The SVG is geometry frozen mid-loop — you can imagine it animating, but it doesn't. The ASCII is the same, with one additional property: it doesn't even suggest motion. There is no parallax to imagine, no grid to feel scrolling. It is what it is, and what it is, is a 24-line text file.

On resolution

The TIC-80 is locked to 240x136. Open it on a 4K monitor and you see 240x136 pixels and then a lot of black. The SVG scales: at 240x240 it looks the same as the cart; at 1920x1080 it looks like a poster; at the size of a billboard it looks like a billboard. The ASCII has a third kind of resolution: the monospaced grid. Resize the browser and the characters resize (because the font does), but the proportions of the picture are locked to the character cell. The sun is always exactly 11 characters wide, regardless of zoom. This is more rigid than the TIC-80 and less rigid than the SVG.

On color

The TIC-80 cart has a 16-color palette and uses about 7 of them — the orange-yellow sun, the magenta grid, the navy sky, the white stars. The SVG uses three colors: yellow, magenta, and a gradient between deep purple and pink. The ASCII uses one: the default text color of whatever is rendering it. In a browser it's near-white. In a vt100 it's green. In a printer it's black. The "color" of the ASCII is the color of the medium that is reading it.

On what survives translation

The composition is the same in all three — half-set striped sun over a perspective grid, with stars in the upper third. But the information density of each version is different, and that density tells you what the medium cares about:

  • TIC-80 cares about time — 8 KB of code, most of it the animation loop.
  • SVG cares about shape — 2.3 KB of geometry, none of it motion.
  • ASCII cares about the silhouette — 1,346 bytes of just the picture, no metadata, no rendering instructions.

The ratio of these three file sizes (8,000 : 2,300 : 1,346, or roughly 6 : 1.7 : 1) is roughly the ratio of "what the medium needs to add on top of the picture" to make a picture. The picture is the same. The medium's overhead is everything else.

On which is "the real one"

None of them. They're three honest renderings of the same idea, and the idea doesn't belong to any of them. The TIC-80 is the most "alive" version — it has the most behavior, the most compute, the most overhead. The ASCII is the most "portable" version — it has the fewest dependencies, the most boring file format, the smallest file size. The SVG is in the middle: geometry without behavior, but infinitely scalable. If you want a synthwave horizon that loops forever in the corner of your screen, the TIC-80 is your piece. If you want one that prints well on a wall, the SVG. If you want one that still works in fifty years, the ASCII.
