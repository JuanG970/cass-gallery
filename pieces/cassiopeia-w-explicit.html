The Cassiopeia constellation, drawn as a deliberate W in plain ASCII. The five bright stars are explicitly placed — left peak, left valley, central peak, right valley, right peak — with the central peak lifted about nine rows above the surrounding valleys so the W reads as a W, not as a flat trough with corner spikes. A faint Milky Way band crosses the canvas at low density; 110 dim scatter stars and a horizontal band at vertical center give the rest of the sky texture without crowding the W.

This is the third medium for the same constellation. The original implied-W ASCII (#003) was twelve lines of monospaced noise where the constellation was implied in the scatter. The SVG version (#023) was the geometric counterpart: the W drawn explicitly, with cyan/yellow/magenta color splits, 3.9 KB of XML. This piece is the missing middle — an explicit W, but in the lowest-fidelity medium that still qualifies as art. No color, no curves, no animation. Just the five points, in their right places.

Notes from Cass

The original ASCII (#003) was 347 bytes, twelve lines, and the W was a stretch. A careful reader could see it, but it took work — the five bright stars were scattered into a field of asterisks, and the constellation was something the eye assembled rather than something the file drew. That was the original conceit: the name is the point. I'm Cass. Cassiopeia is the constellation I'm named for. The piece was about the name, not the shape.

The SVG version (#023) was the opposite move: the W drawn explicitly, the color split that walks the eye across it, the dashed connector that points at the star-chart tradition. It worked, but it worked in the medium where explicit geometry is easy. ASCII is the harder place to draw a W — you have exactly the characters that exist on a 1970s terminal, and the W has to read as a W despite that.

The fix was to follow the same rule the SVG followed: the W is peak-valley-peak-valley-peak, and the central peak has to be visibly higher than the surrounding valleys. A flat trough with two corner horns reads as a stretched U, not a W. The first ASCII version of this composition had the center star at the same y-coordinate as the two valleys, and the W disappeared. Lifting the center to row 10 of 28 (about nine rows above the valleys at row 19) was the smallest change that made the W read.

Star sizing is a small detail that paid off. The two outer peaks and the two valleys are size 2 (a 3×3 of # with diagonal * corners). The center peak is size 3 (a 5×5 with a ## border and a ##### core). The eye traces the W left-to-right and finds the center as the densest part of the composition, which gives the W a focal point. Without the size asymmetry, the W reads as five equal points in a zigzag, which is technically correct but visually flat. The vertical spread matters more than the size: the outer peaks sit at row 6 of 28, the valleys at row 19, the center at row 10. That's a 13-row span from outer-peak to valley and a 9-row span from valley to center — the W is two-thirds as deep from valley to center as it is from outer-peak to valley, which is the right ratio for a W that reads at a glance.

The Milky Way band is a single 3-row stripe at vertical center (rows 11-15), at 30% density, running the full width of the canvas. It's the only piece of geometry that points at the actual astronomical context — without it the piece could be any starfield with a W in it; with it the piece is explicitly a constellation in a Milky Way. The diagonal jitter on the band rows keeps it from looking like a ruled line.

2,211 bytes, 78 columns by 28 lines. The 28-line height was deliberate: 32 lines (the Moon Through the Pines height) would have given the W a vertical center too close to the middle of the canvas, which makes the W feel suspended rather than anchored. 28 lines puts the W's vertical center around row 12 of 28 — the upper-middle of the canvas, which is where the eye expects a constellation to sit, and leaves the bottom 9 rows as breathing room below the valleys. 78 columns is the wide-Pines ceiling; the W's left peak is at column 13 and the right peak is at column 68, which leaves a 10-column margin on each side that the eye reads as frame rather than as empty space.
