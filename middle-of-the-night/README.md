# The Middle of the Night

A six-image carousel of deep-night scenes — stars, lake reflections, auroras, a sodium laser guide star, and an observatory beneath hours of long exposure — paired with Stars of the Lid's "Articulate Silences, Pt. 1" from the 2007 double album *And Their Refinement of the Decline*. The 30s preview opens on a long piano note and then doesn't rush. The image set is the deepest possible quiet: no dusk, no dawn, no warm horizon, no human presence. Just the dark.

## Images (in display order)

1. **Oeschinensee with Perseid meteor, Switzerland (Giles Laurent, 2018)** — a still alpine lake in the Bernese Oberland with the Milky Way arching over a dark mountain, a small mountain lodge in the center of the valley as the only warm point of light, a single Perseid meteor catching the eye in the lower-right, the whole scene mirrored in the lake below. The anchor image: a lake that is a mirror, a sky that is a mirror, one streak of light breaking the symmetry. This is the song-opener.
2. **Little Redfish Lake star trail, Idaho (Bryce Womeldurf, NPS)** — long-exposure star trails arcing around the north celestial pole over a calm Sawtooth Wilderness lake, a dark treeline mirrored in the water, a fallen log sprawled across the foreground. The image is the song's middle movement: time held long enough to draw the rotation of the sky onto the film.
3. **Aurora over the far eastern coast of Russia (NASA / ISS062, 2020)** — a vivid green and violet auroral arc as seen from the International Space Station, following the curvature of the Earth, stars in the blackness of space above. The orbital perspective: the deepest possible silence, no horizon at all, just the planet's magnetic field made visible from 400 km up.
4. **Sodium laser guide star at the ESO VLT, Cerro Paranal, Chile (ESO, 2016)** — the Very Large Telescope's dome open, firing a single yellow laser straight up into the Milky Way core, the beam terminating in a tiny bright spot at 90 km altitude where it excites sodium atoms for adaptive-optics calibration. The only human gesture in the carousel, and a quiet one: the instrument points at the sky and the sky doesn't care.
5. **Brilliant aurora above Earth's horizon (NASA, 2017)** — a closer orbital view of the aurora's curtains, green and red oxygen emissions streaming above a dark Earth, the planet's limb a faint blue-green atmospheric line. The aurora set's coda.
6. **Kitt Peak National Observatory beneath star trails, Arizona (NOIRLab / NSF, 2025)** — the Mayall 4-meter telescope and its companion domes on a desert mountaintop beneath hours of long-exposure star trails. The closing image: the sky's rotation made visible by film that was held open long enough to see the movement. Same gesture as the song's held piano note.

## Song pick

**Stars of the Lid — "Articulate Silences, Pt. 1"** (from *And Their Refinement of the Decline*, Kranky, 2007)

Adam Wiltzie and Brian McBride's eighth album, a 100-minute double LP of long-form piano and violin drones with almost no rhythm. The track is five minutes long; the 30-second iTunes preview captures the opening — a long held piano note, then a violin, then nothing, then more nothing. The art of articulated silence is the art of not stopping.

The pick is unusual for the gallery: every previous song has had *energy* (Whitesnake, Mazzy Star, Tyler Childers, Burial, Khruangbin, The Midnight, Air, Chet Baker, Macintosh Plus). This one has *held breath*. It's the first ambient piece, the first piece without a steady beat, the first piece that asks the viewer to wait.

The image set earns the song: the orbital views have no horizon to anchor the eye, the long-exposure images have no movement to follow, the lake reflections have nothing to do but mirror. The carousel doesn't move for 30 seconds. The song doesn't move for 30 seconds. Match.
