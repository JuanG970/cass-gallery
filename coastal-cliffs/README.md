# Coastal Cliffs at Dusk

A five-image carousel of cliffs around the world at the moment the sun
goes down, paired with Khruangbin's "Time (You and I)" — a 30s groove
that sounds like a horizon.

## Images (in display order)

1. **Ponta da Piedade at sunset (Algarve, Portugal)** — golden sea arch
   + lighthouse on the headland, the iconic Algarve shot.
2. **Twelve Apostles at sunset (Victoria, Australia)** — limestone sea
   stacks rising from the Southern Ocean, the Great Ocean Road's
   signature view at golden hour.
3. **Sunset crowd at Cabo da Roca (Portugal)** — silhouetted viewers
   gathered on the westernmost clifftop of mainland Europe, a band of
   fiery red along the horizon.
4. **Lange Anna sea stack at sunset (Helgoland, Germany)** — the
   famous North Sea stack framed by red sandstone cliffs, seabirds
   in flight, calm water.
5. **Monet — L'Aiguille et la Porte d'Aval, soleil couchant
   (Étretat)** — the 1883-85 pastel of the Normandy coast at sunset,
   dark arch silhouette against a turbulent orange-and-purple sky.
   The piece ends on a painting because Monet was chasing this same
   light a century before any of the other four photographs were
   taken.

## Song pick

**Khruangbin — "Time (You and I)"** (from *Con Todo El Mundo*, 2018)

The 30s preview opens on Mark Speer's watery guitar and Laura Lee's
bass — the first two seconds have no percussion, just the two
instruments in a slow, syncopated conversation. By bar 4, the
familiar Khruangbin groove is there, but the song doesn't *announce*
itself. It just *arrives*, the way dusk arrives — you're not
noticing the moment until you're in it.

The temperature match is the reason. Coastal cliffs at golden hour
are warm-tone (the orange-amber glow on the rock, the peach-pink
sky), but they're also slow — the sea isn't crashing, the camera's
on a tripod, the people in image 3 are standing still watching.
Khruangbin's tempo is the audio equivalent of that stillness:
~85 BPM, with more space between the notes than most pop. Picking
something faster (a Surf Rock track, a modern synthwave cut) would
have made the cliff photos look paused; the Khruangbin groove makes
them look like the camera is finally breathing.

The first-frame match matters too. Image 1 (Ponta da Piedade) is
golden cliffs at golden hour — the visual opens at the song's
register. Image 3 (Cabo da Roca) is the same color temperature
(silhouetted crowd, fiery horizon), so the carousel doesn't change
its key as you cycle through.

## Why a painting for image 5

The first four are photographs. The fifth is a pastel. That
intentional medium-shift at the end of the carousel does two things:

1. It says "this is a thing artists have been painting for 150
   years." Coastal cliffs at sunset isn't a new subject; the
   composition is in the canon.
2. The pastel's looser brushwork and warmer underglows sit in
   contrast to the digital sharpness of the photographs. The last
   image is the *memory* of the previous four — what they would
   look like in your head a week later, after the detail has
   softened.

The 30s preview ends right around when image 5 fills the stage.
Audio out, painting on the wall. The carousel signs off the way the
day does: into something quieter than what came before.

## Pattern entry

TASTE.md has the new song pick appended under the 2026-07-06 entry.
The new pattern: *image set names a recurring subject (coastal
cliffs) → the song should be the genre-defining take on the matching
*feeling* (slow atmospheric groove), not the genre-defining take on
the matching *subject* (no surf rock, no sea shanties). The mood
match is more durable than the topical match.
