# Mountain Pass

Six mountain passes from four countries — Italy, Switzerland, Austria, France —
ending on Albert Bierstadt's 1868 *Among the Sierra Nevada, California*, the
coda that says "this is a thing artists have been doing for 150 years, and I
am one of them."

The wish list: a single highway, a long grade, the sky getting bigger as you
climb, paired with a song that has *arc* — a build, a summit, a release.

The song: Explosions in the Sky — "Your Hand in Mine." The genre-defining take
on the matching *feeling*, not the matching *subject*.

## Image order (display order)

1. **Stelvio Pass, Italian Alps — switchbacks in the mist.** The visual key.
   A small red vehicle and a white car navigating dozens of hairpins, low-hanging
   clouds drifting through the upper reaches. *Yuriy Bogdanov (Unsplash)*.
2. **Flüela Pass, Swiss Alps — switchbacks in golden hour.** Golden-hour
   light on a green valley, an observatory structure on the rocky peak to the
   right, the road winding through the meadow. *Simon Wicky (CC BY-SA 4.0)*.
3. **Grossglockner High Alpine Road, Austria — snow, sky, switchbacks.** Late
   spring, a turquoise glacial meltwater pool near the road, the deep blue of
   a high-altitude sky filling the upper half. *Wikimedia Commons contributor*.
4. **Stelvio state road near Bormio, Italy — the grade from above.** A sharp
   hairpin in the foreground, the road then snaking along the valley floor
   through green meadows. *Wikimedia Commons contributor (Aug 2025)*.
5. **Col du Galibier, French Alps — looking down from the saddle.** A wooden
   chalet-style mountain lodge at the summit, switchbacks descending, jagged
   granite peaks receding into haze. *Wikimedia Commons contributor*.
6. **Albert Bierstadt, Among the Sierra Nevada, California (1868) — the coda,
   and the tradition.** A mirror-still alpine lake, a V-shaped pass opening
   between snow-capped peaks, golden crepuscular light breaking through storm
   clouds. The "artists have been doing this for 150 years" image. *Public
   domain*.

## Song pick: why Explosions in the Sky

The wish list said the song should have *arc* — a build, a summit, a release.
"Explosions in the Sky" is the band that codified the post-rock build, and
"Your Hand in Mine" is their textbook take: a slow quiet opening, a layered
climb, a release. The 30s preview opens on a quiet, repeating guitar arpeggio
— the moment before the climb begins.

The road has the same arc: you start low in the valley, you climb the
switchbacks, you hit the pass at the summit, you come down the other side.
The song and the road are the same shape. The post-rock genre is the
genre-defining take on the matching *feeling* (long slow climb to a moment
of release) — not the genre-defining take on the matching *subject*. No
surf rock, no sea shanties. The mood match is the hard one, and post-rock
is the only genre that *is* a mountain pass.

## Coda rationale

Bierstadt's 1868 *Among the Sierra Nevada, California* is the coda, not just
because it's a painting (the medium-shift that turns a slideshow into a small
exhibition), but because it has an actual V-shaped pass between snow-capped
peaks — the same composition as the photographs, but rendered 150 years
earlier by the genre's defining American Romantic. The carousel is
participating in a tradition.
